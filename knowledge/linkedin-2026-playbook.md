# LinkedIn in early 2026: a data-driven playbook for Dr Ryo Eguchi

**LinkedIn's algorithm now runs on a 150-billion-parameter AI model called 360Brew that rewards topical expertise, dwell time, and conversation depth over raw engagement volume — a structural shift that actually favours small, focused profiles like Ryo's over broad audiences.** The platform's organic reach has dropped roughly 50% year-over-year, but the decline disproportionately affects company pages and generic content. For a GP-turned-founder posting niche health tech content to a tight network of 136 connections, this algorithm change creates a genuine window: LinkedIn now distributes content to the *right* people rather than the most people. What follows is a research synthesis drawing primarily on Richard van der Blom's Algorithm InSights 2025 report (1.8 million posts analysed), Trust Insights' Q1 2026 algorithm guide, Buffer's 2-million-post dataset, the Edelman-LinkedIn 2025 B2B Thought Leadership Impact Report, and multiple practitioner case studies from the health tech space.

## 1. The algorithm has fundamentally changed since mid-2025

LinkedIn replaced its legacy engagement-based ranking system with two AI-powered stages. The first, a Causal LLM retrieval system deployed in an October 2025 update, narrows roughly 300 million candidate posts down to ~2,000 per user using semantic embeddings. The second, **360Brew** — a decoder-only foundation model published as a research paper on arXiv and confirmed in active production by Trust Insights' Q1 2026 guide — scores those candidates using 2–3 months of each user's activity history, profile data, and interaction patterns to generate personalised relevance scores.

The practical consequences are significant. A new **"Depth Score"** metric (documented by Digital Applied, February 2026) has replaced vanity engagement as the primary distribution signal. Its components, weighted in descending order: **dwell time** (how long readers spend before scrolling), **comment depth** (multi-reply threads carry far more weight than single reactions), **saves** (estimated at 5–10× the algorithmic weight of a like per van der Blom's data), and **private shares via DM** (a high-intent signal). Likes alone now carry minimal weight.

Posts now go through a three-phase evaluation. Phase 1: a quality classifier assesses spam, content substance, and author credibility within minutes. Phase 2: early engagement patterns in the first 60–90 minutes — the "golden hour" — determine initial distribution. Phase 3, which is new: the Depth Score accumulates over 24–48 hours and can expand or contract distribution well beyond the original window. LinkedIn VP of Product Gyanda Sachdeva confirmed in mid-June 2025 that the platform intentionally surfaces older posts (sometimes 2–3 weeks old), testing what she called "a new sort of efficient frontier" between recency and relevance.

Two critical shifts for Ryo specifically: **360Brew cross-references post topics against your profile and posting history.** Sticking to 2–3 core topics (e.g., primary care technology, NZ healthcare innovation, founder journey) for 90+ days allows the algorithm to identify your natural audience with precision. Jumping between unrelated topics — leadership Monday, coding Tuesday — actively confuses the model and reduces distribution. Second, **keywords in natural copy have replaced hashtags** as the discovery mechanism. Van der Blom's data shows posts with more than 3 hashtags had **70% lower reach** than posts with none.

## 2. Growing from 136 to 500+ connections in 90 days is achievable with disciplined outreach

The maths works. Sending 80–100 connection requests per week (the safe limit for free accounts on a rolling 7-day window) at a **35–45% acceptance rate** yields 28–45 new connections per week, or roughly 120–180 per month. At that pace, the 364-connection gap closes in **8–12 weeks**. Sales Navigator users with high Social Selling Index scores can push to 200 requests per week, compressing the timeline further.

**The personalised-note debate is settled, and the answer is counterintuitive.** ReactIn's analysis of ~80,000 connection requests found blank invites achieved **55–68% acceptance rates** versus 28–45% for templated notes. LeadLoft's test on Y Combinator founders showed no-note requests had **89% higher odds** of acceptance. The reason: most notes read as sales pitches, triggering defensive filtering. The exception is high-value targets — C-suite executives, investors, and PHO decision-makers who receive heavy inbound — where a hyper-personalised note referencing shared context (a comment exchange, mutual connection, or specific post) lifts acceptance rates to 45–60%. For Ryo's strategy: **send blank requests to peers, clinicians, and mid-level health tech contacts; write personalised notes only when connecting with investors, PHO leadership, or named individuals you've already engaged with.**

The warm-up sequence matters enormously. Viewing a prospect's profile, then liking or commenting on their post, then waiting 2–3 days before requesting connection reportedly **triples acceptance rates** compared to cold requests (Gracker.ai, 2025). This aligns with LinkedIn's new "Volume Tax" — high-volume activity with low acceptance rates is penalised, while selectivity is rewarded.

Critical safety limits to avoid restrictions: keep pending (unaccepted) requests under **500** (hard cap at 700); spread requests evenly across the week at 15–20 per day rather than batching; withdraw unaccepted requests after 2–3 weeks. For Ryo's account, which has been relatively dormant in outreach, a warm-up protocol of 5–10 requests per day in week 1, scaling to 15–20 by week 3, prevents triggering LinkedIn's new-activity spike detection.

For targeting relevant connections — investors, PHOs, health tech peers — LinkedIn's AI-powered natural language search (launched November 2025) allows queries like "health tech investors in New Zealand" or "PHO innovation leaders Auckland" without Boolean syntax. Sales Navigator remains the gold standard with 40+ advanced filters including "Posted on LinkedIn in last 30 days," which identifies active users far more likely to accept and engage.

## 3. Document carousels and polls are the underused formats with the highest ROI

Ryo has been using only text and single-image posts. The data strongly suggests diversifying — particularly because **single-image posts now underperform text-only content by 30%** in the 2026 algorithm, reversing the 2024–2025 trend.

Current engagement rates by format, synthesised across SocialInsider's 1-million-post benchmark study, van der Blom's 1.8-million-post dataset, and Digital Applied's February 2026 analysis:

| Format | Engagement rate | Reach multiplier | % of all LinkedIn content |
|---|---|---|---|
| Multi-image carousel / PDF document | **6.6%** | 1.45× | Low (underused) |
| Native video | 5.6% | 1.10× | Growing fast (+69% YoY) |
| Polls | 4.4% | **1.64×** (highest) | **1.4%** (massively underused) |
| Text-only | 4.0% | 1.0× (baseline) | Declining (–41% usage YoY) |
| Single image | 4.85% | Below text-only in 2026 | **58%** of all content (oversaturated) |
| Link posts | Lowest | Penalty (~60% reach reduction) | 30% of brand posts |

The opportunity is clearest in two formats. **Document/carousel posts** generate the highest dwell time (each swipe counts as an interaction), drive the most saves (the algorithm's "super-signal"), and achieve 2.5× more shares than other formats. Optimal length is **8–10 slides** — van der Blom's data shows the algorithm penalises low completion rates, so overly long carousels that users abandon hurt rather than help. For Ryo, a carousel titled "5 things GPs wish health tech founders understood" or a step-by-step framework for patient data management would align with his expertise and generate saves.

**Polls** represent the single most underexploited format — just 1.4% of all LinkedIn content, yet they carry the highest reach multiplier at 1.64×. Every vote pushes the poll back into the voter's network, creating a viral feedback loop particularly powerful for small audiences. A well-crafted poll from Ryo — "What's the biggest tech frustration in your GP practice?" — would generate engagement from his exact target audience while providing genuine market research data he could reference in future content.

Native video is growing (+45% YoY in uploads) and getting algorithmic support through LinkedIn's dedicated Video Tab, but the initial aggressive boost from 2025 has normalised. Short videos under 60 seconds with captions (80% watch muted) perform best, and brand appearance in the first 4 seconds yields a +69% performance boost. For Ryo, a 45-second video walking through a ClinicPro feature at his desk — casual, not polished — would differentiate him from the text-heavy health tech content ecosystem.

## 4. Strategic commenting is the single highest-leverage growth tactic for small networks

The data on commenting is unambiguous and dramatic. **Commenting on 10–20 posts daily can boost profile views by up to 50%** and increase engagement on your own posts by approximately 10% (Social Pilot data, 2025). People you comment on are **3× more likely to engage with your future posts**, creating a compounding network effect.

Van der Blom's reciprocity data shows the algorithmic mechanics clearly: commenting once on a creator's post creates an **80% chance** you'll see their next post, while sending a DM creates a 90% chance. The algorithm treats engagement as a bilateral signal — it doesn't just boost the post you commented on, it trains LinkedIn to show *your* content to that person and their network.

Quality thresholds matter enormously. An A/B test documented in November 2025 compared two posts: Post A received 43 mostly short, generic comments; Post B received 19 detailed, threaded comments. **Post B reached 3.2× more people and drove 8× more profile visits.** The minimum quality bar is **15+ words** that meaningfully contribute to the topic — van der Blom's research treats this as the threshold for "meaningful engagement." A single 150-word expert exchange can outperform 50 superficial "Great post!" interactions. Comments generated by AI tools receive **5× less response** from post authors and **7× less** from audiences.

The optimal routine, supported by multiple practitioner datasets:

- **Comment on 5–10 relevant posts for 15–30 minutes BEFORE publishing your own content.** This signals active community membership to the algorithm, "warming" it in your favour. Multiple sources recommend engaging with at least 15 posts before publishing.
- **After publishing, engage with at least 5 more posts** to maintain the activity signal.
- **Respond to ALL comments on your own post within the first hour** — responding within 15 minutes reportedly generates a significant algorithmic boost, as this first hour is make-or-break for distribution.
- **Never be the first to comment on your own post** — van der Blom's data shows this can limit reach.

For Ryo, the highest-value commenting targets would be posts from NZ health system leaders, health tech investors, PHO innovation officers, and larger health tech founders. Charlie Hunt, co-founder of The Lime, documented a **305% increase in impressions** and **483% increase in content performance** over 90 days through strategic commenting alone — a directly applicable case study for Ryo's situation.

## 6. Health tech thought leadership that works for small audiences

Column Content's analysis of 20 MedTech companies found that **leadership-driven content outperforms corporate updates by 30–50%** on LinkedIn. The most compelling case study is Alafair Biosciences: their CEO's LinkedIn presence achieved a **21,058% increase in profile impressions**, with company content hitting 61,570 impressions in four months. The strategy that drove this was shifting from product-focused content to leadership-driven storytelling about *why* the product mattered.

For a GP founder like Ryo, five content angles consistently outperform based on MedTech and health tech practitioner data:

- **Regulatory and system analysis with implications** — don't just state what changed in the NZ health system; explain what it means for practices, patients, and technology adoption. This signals deep domain expertise.
- **"Building in public" milestone updates** — sharing ClinicPro's development journey, including setbacks and lessons. In the VC world, "a well-crafted build-in-public thread can do more than a pitch deck" (Marktal, 2025).
- **Patient-outcome narratives** — stories from general practice (appropriately anonymised) that illustrate the problem ClinicPro solves. The 73% of most successful health tech content marketers use case studies (Content Marketing Institute).
- **Contrarian takes on health tech** — "Everyone thinks AI will replace GPs. Here's why that misses the point entirely." Contrarian hooks generate the most comments, which the algorithm weights at 15× a like.
- **Practical frameworks** — "The 3-question test I use before recommending any health tech to my practice." Frameworks drive saves, the algorithm's super-signal.

For hooks, three patterns dominate in 2026. **The data hook**: "We tracked 200 patient appointments across 3 platforms. One outperformed by 40%." **The contrarian hook**: "Most health tech startups build for hospitals. That's why they fail." **The narrative hook**: "A patient walked in 20 minutes before close. What happened next changed how I think about clinic software." The first 140 characters must compel the "see more" click — write the hook last, after drafting the full post.

The Edelman-LinkedIn 2025 B2B Thought Leadership Impact Report (surveying nearly 2,000 professionals) found that **95% of "hidden decision-makers"** — people with buying influence who never interact with sales teams — say strong thought leadership makes them more receptive to outreach. For investor credibility specifically, Sendoso's data shows **120% higher closed-won deal sizes** when prospects follow Director+ executives on LinkedIn.

## 7. Optimal posting times for NZ and APAC audiences

The consensus across Buffer (1 million+ posts, February 2026), Sprout Social (2.7 billion engagements), and Hootsuite/Critical Truth (1 million+ posts) points to **Tuesday through Thursday** as the strongest days, with peak engagement at **8–10 AM in the audience's local time zone**.

For New Zealand specifically, LiSeller's APAC analysis and SocialChamp's 2026 Australian data converge on **8:00 AM, 10:00 AM, and 2:00 PM** as the three engagement peaks across the Australia/NZ region. Since NZ is 1–2 hours ahead of AEST, Ryo should aim to post at **8–10 AM NZST** to catch both the NZ morning audience and the early Australian browsing window. If his primary audience is NZ-based (PHOs, local health system), **10 AM NZST on Tuesday or Thursday** is the optimal slot.

A notable shift from 2024: van der Blom's 2025 data shows peak times have moved slightly earlier (7–10 AM versus the prior 8–11 AM window). Content lifespan has also extended — posts generating genuine conversation can remain visible for 2–3 weeks rather than the previous 24-hour focus. This means a Tuesday morning post that sparks a comment thread can still be surfacing in feeds the following weekend.

**Consistency matters more than perfect timing.** LinkedIn's algorithm treats consistent posting profiles as "active and trustworthy," expanding initial distribution on each new post. Pages posting weekly see **5.6× more follower growth** than those posting less often. For Ryo, committing to the same 2–3 days per week at the same time will train both the algorithm and his audience.

One important constraint: avoid posting the same format back-to-back (e.g., two carousels in a row), which can suppress performance by **up to 20%**. The recommended rhythm: text post Tuesday, carousel Thursday, poll or video the following Tuesday.
