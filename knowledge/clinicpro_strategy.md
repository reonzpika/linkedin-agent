## Content Format and Structure

### Default format: Text posts

**Why text:**
- Fastest to produce
- Age well (no broken images or outdated data)
- Force a real point of view
- LinkedIn algorithm doesn't penalise text posts

**Structure that works (based on 2025 algorithm research):**
1. **Strong hook (first 1-2 sentences):** Grab attention with specific observation or bold statement. The first two lines determine whether someone clicks "see more."
2. **Build to systemic point (1-2 paragraphs):** Connect specific observation to broader pattern.
3. **Your take (1 paragraph):** What this means, why it matters, what needs to change.
4. **No open-ended questions:** End with your conclusion, not "what do you think?"

**Length:** 150-300 words optimal. Algorithm favours "dwell time" (how long people spend reading). Longer posts that keep people reading outperform short posts they scroll past.

**Example structure (Pillar 1 infrastructure post):**
```
I tried to send a clinical photo to dermatology yesterday. 
Medtech Cloud doesn't support direct image uploads to referrals yet.

So the workaround is: resize manually, upload to referral as attachment, hope it doesn't bounce for file size, then chase up when specialist says they can't open it.

This is the gap nobody talks about when discussing "seamless cloud migration." The technical upgrade is real. The workflow friction is also real. And GPs just absorb it as normal.

Medtech's new ALEX Intelligence Layer could change this; their recent API updates finally allow gathering provider inbox results, which is a huge step. But the last-mile problem of actually getting clinical content where it needs to go hasn't been solved yet.

Until then, it's manual workarounds. Every referral.
```

**Why this works:**
- Opens with specific, relatable problem
- Names the technical issue clearly
- Acknowledges Medtech's progress (positive, not complaining)
- Ends with reality check, not question
- 140 words (scannable, holds attention)

### Occasional format: Single image with text

**When to use:**
- Workflow comparisons (old vs new)
- Before/after visualisations
- Data visualisations
- Process diagrams

**Your Referral Images workflow post is a perfect example.** Clear visual comparison, minimal text, makes the point immediately.

**How to create:**
- Keep images simple (no walls of text on image)
- Use consistent colour scheme (blues/whites, medical + modern aesthetic)
- Make sure text is readable on mobile
- Post the image, add short caption (100-150 words)

**Don't use images just for engagement.** Only use when visual genuinely communicates something text can't.

### Occasional format: Carousel posts (multi-image swipes)

**When to use sparingly:**
- Step-by-step breakdowns that genuinely need multiple frames
- Comparison tables
- Tutorial-style content

**Why to limit carousels:**
- Time-intensive to produce well
- A weak carousel is worse than a strong text post
- Algorithm now tracks "consumption rate"; if people swipe to slide 2 and bail, that's a negative signal
- Better to save carousels for high-value content (quarterly roundup, major announcement)

**Current recommendation:** Stick with text + occasional single image. Add carousels only when content genuinely requires that format.

### Video: Not a priority

**Why:**
- Time-intensive to produce well
- Network too small to justify investment yet
- Text posts with strong POV outperform mediocre videos
- Save video for when network exceeds 500 connections and posts consistently get 500+ impressions

## The Golden Hour Rule (Critical)

LinkedIn tests each post on a small sample of your network in the first 60 minutes. If engagement is strong, it expands reach to second and third-degree connections. If weak, the post dies.

**Practical implications:**

1. **Post when you have 15-20 minutes to engage.**
2. **Before posting:** Comment on 5-6 other posts from your target audience (warms up the algorithm, shows you're active).
3. **After posting:** Respond to every comment within first hour. Treat the post like a live event.
4. **Don't tag people hoping for engagement.** Tag only when legitimately relevant (discussing their work, their platform, their policy).
5. **Put links in first comment, never post body.** LinkedIn reduces initial reach by ~30% for posts with outbound links.

**Example golden hour workflow:**

7:45am - Comment on 5-6 posts from GPs, practice managers, health tech founders  
8:00am - Publish scheduled post  
8:00-8:20am - Monitor for comments, respond immediately to anyone who engages  
8:20am - Post any relevant link in first comment  
8:30am - Check back, respond to any new comments  

**Why this matters:**
With 136 connections, algorithm shows post to maybe 15-20 people initially. If 3-4 engage quickly, algorithm expands. If zero engage, post dies. Your active participation in first hour signals to algorithm that post is worth amplifying.

## What Not to Do (Algorithm Penalty Triggers)

LinkedIn's 2025 algorithm actively penalises:

1. **Engagement baiting:** Asking "what do you think?" or similar questions to force comments
2. **Link stuffing:** Outbound links in post body (reduces initial reach by 30%)
3. **Excessive hashtags:** More than 5 hashtags per post
4. **Irrelevant tagging:** Tagging people without genuine reason
5. **Posting too frequently:** Less than 12 hours between posts
6. **Low-quality content:** Posts with grammar errors, spam-like formatting
7. **Overpromising:** Mentioning features that don't exist yet
8. **Engagement pods:** Coordinated likes/comments from same group (algorithm detects this)

**Don't:**
- Open posts with product name or update
- Ask open-ended questions about workflow problems (invites competitors)
- Put links in post body (first comment only)
- Post more than once per week until network is bigger
- Use more than 4 hashtags
- Tag people without genuine reason

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

### Pitfall 1: Posting without engaging
**Problem:** Publishing post and disappearing. No comments, no responses, no interaction.  
**Result:** Algorithm sees no engagement, kills post immediately.  
**Solution:** Treat every post like a live event. Be present for golden hour.

### Pitfall 2: Asking for engagement explicitly
**Problem:** "What do you think?" or "Share your experience" at end of posts.  
**Result:** Algorithm flags as engagement bait, reduces reach.  
**Solution:** End with your take, not a question. Let engagement happen naturally.

### Pitfall 3: Over-promoting product
**Problem:** Every post mentions ClinicPro or leads with product update.  
**Result:** Looks sales-y, people tune out, network doesn't grow.  
**Solution:** 70% of posts should not mention ClinicPro at all. Product mention is earned by the story, not the point.

### Pitfall 4: Comparing to competitors
**Problem:** "Unlike MedImage..." or "Better than QuickShot..."  
**Result:** Looks defensive, invites competitor advocates into comments.  
**Solution:** Talk about the problem and your approach. Ignore competitors publicly.

### Pitfall 5: Inconsistent posting
**Problem:** Post 3 times one week, nothing for 3 weeks, then 2 posts.  
**Result:** Algorithm doesn't build topic authority, network doesn't know what to expect.  
**Solution:** Once per week, same day/time, for 12 weeks minimum. Consistency matters more than volume.

### Pitfall 6: Wrong metrics focus
**Problem:** Obsessing over likes and impressions instead of meaningful engagement.  
**Result:** Chasing vanity metrics, missing the signals that matter.  
**Solution:** Track: comments from target audience, connection requests from relevant people, inbound DMs, profile views. These predict business outcomes.

### Pitfall 7: Talking to everyone
**Problem:** Generic posts trying to appeal to GPs, investors, developers, patients simultaneously.  
**Result:** Posts resonate with no one, network stays small.  
**Solution:** Every post should be clearly for one segment of target audience. Rotate segments, don't blend them.

The goal is not to become a LinkedIn influencer. The goal is to build a network and credibility that makes ClinicPro's go-to-market easier when the time comes.

Every post should either:
1. Demonstrate insider knowledge (you understand NZ primary care infrastructure)
2. Show technical capability (you can build production software)
3. Establish clinical credibility (you're a practising GP, not an outside observer)

If a post doesn't do at least one of those three things, don't publish it.

Quality, consistency, and patience. That's the formula.
