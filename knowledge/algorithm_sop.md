# LinkedIn Algorithm Standard Operating Procedure (2025)

**Source:** ClinicPro LinkedIn Strategy  
**Purpose:** 2025 algorithm rules, content structure, posting cadence, Golden Hour protocol, hashtag and tagging strategy.

---

## Content Format and Structure

### Default format: Text posts

**Why text:**
- Fastest to produce
- Age well (no broken images or outdated data)
- Force a real point of view
- LinkedIn algorithm doesn't penalise text posts

**Structure that works (based on 2025 algorithm research):**
1. **Strong hook (first 1-2 sentences):** Grab attention with specific observation or bold statement. The first two lines determine whether someone clicks "see more."
2. **Build to systemic point (1-2 paragraphs):** Connect specific observation to broader pattern.
3. **Your take (1 paragraph):** What this means, why it matters, what needs to change.
4. **No open-ended questions:** End with your conclusion, not "what do you think?"

**Length:** 150-300 words optimal. Algorithm favours "dwell time" (how long people spend reading). Longer posts that keep people reading outperform short posts they scroll past.

**Example structure (Pillar 1 infrastructure post):**
```
I tried to send a clinical photo to dermatology yesterday. 
Medtech Cloud doesn't support direct image uploads to referrals yet.

So the workaround is: resize manually, upload to referral as attachment, hope it doesn't bounce for file size, then chase up when specialist says they can't open it.

This is the gap nobody talks about when discussing "seamless cloud migration." The technical upgrade is real. The workflow friction is also real. And GPs just absorb it as normal.

Medtech's new ALEX Intelligence Layer could change this; their recent API updates finally allow gathering provider inbox results, which is a huge step. But the last-mile problem of actually getting clinical content where it needs to go hasn't been solved yet.

Until then, it's manual workarounds. Every referral.
```

**Why this works:**
- Opens with specific, relatable problem
- Names the technical issue clearly
- Acknowledges Medtech's progress (positive, not complaining)
- Ends with reality check, not question
- 140 words (scannable, holds attention)

## Posting Cadence and Timing

### Frequency: Once per week

**Why once per week:**
- Sustainable alongside clinical work and product development
- Quality of one strong post per week beats two rushed posts
- Small network can't support higher frequency without splitting engagement
- Consistent enough for algorithm to recognise topic authority

**Research findings:**
- Posting 2x weekly to small network splits engagement and kills both posts
- Algorithm needs concentrated engagement in first 60 minutes
- Better to build network first, then increase frequency
- Once network exceeds 300 relevant connections, reassess frequency

**When to increase frequency:**
- After 500+ relevant connections
- After posts consistently generate 500+ impressions in first hour
- After inbound DMs become regular occurrence
- Not before

### Best posting times for NZ audience

**Tuesday-Thursday, 8-10am NZST**

**Why:**
- GPs check LinkedIn early morning before clinic or during breaks
- Practice managers check LinkedIn morning
- Tuesday-Thursday avoid Monday chaos and Friday wind-down
- Algorithm tests posts on small segment first; need that segment online when post goes live

**Global research says Tuesday-Thursday 9-11am ET performs best, but optimise for NZ audience since they're your primary market.**

**How to handle scheduling:**
- Schedule posts using LinkedIn native scheduler or Buffer
- Be available 15-20 minutes after post goes live to respond to early comments
- Don't post and disappear (kills algorithmic momentum)

## The Golden Hour Rule (Critical)

LinkedIn tests each post on a small sample of your network in the first 60 minutes. If engagement is strong, it expands reach to second and third-degree connections. If weak, the post dies.

**Practical implications:**

1. **Post when you have 15-20 minutes to engage.**
2. **Before posting:** Comment on 5-6 other posts from your target audience (warms up the algorithm, shows you're active).
3. **After posting:** Respond to every comment within first hour. Treat the post like a live event.
4. **Don't tag people hoping for engagement.** Tag only when legitimately relevant (discussing their work, their platform, their policy).
5. **Put links in first comment, never post body.** LinkedIn reduces initial reach by ~30% for posts with outbound links.

**Example golden hour workflow:**

7:45am - Comment on 5-6 posts from GPs, practice managers, health tech founders  
8:00am - Publish scheduled post  
8:00-8:20am - Monitor for comments, respond immediately to anyone who engages  
8:20am - Post any relevant link in first comment  
8:30am - Check back, respond to any new comments  

**Why this matters:**
With 136 connections, algorithm shows post to maybe 15-20 people initially. If 3-4 engage quickly, algorithm expands. If zero engage, post dies. Your active participation in first hour signals to algorithm that post is worth amplifying.

**Comments over 15 words carry 2.5x more algorithmic weight than shorter interactions.**

### Golden Hour comment guidelines

**Length:** 15-25 words (one substantial sentence, or two short sentences maximum)

**Tone:** Simple, everyday English. Share your own practice experience. No formal vocabulary or essay rhythm.

**Good examples:**
- "We're seeing similar productivity gains at our practice since adding Heidi. The hard part was getting buy-in from the older GPs initially."
- "This is honest. The GP funding model makes it really hard to justify new tools, even when they save time."
- "The 15-minute documentation time is the real issue. If the note is faster, you can actually think during the consult."
- "We hit this exact problem last month. Ended up calling the specialist directly to confirm they got the referral."

**Bad examples (too formal, too long):**
- "Getting Heidi into every NZ ED is a genuine infrastructure achievement, and the Middlemore productivity data backs it up. The implementation challenge you're describing..."
- "The system rewarding throughput over thinking is a documentation problem as much as a culture problem. When the note takes 15 minutes..."

**Banned phrases:**
- "genuine infrastructure achievement", "structural headwinds", "documentation tax", "path of least resistance", "irreducible feature", "curation problem", "identity shift", "doesn't fully resolve"
- Any phrase that sounds like a policy brief or academic paper

**Structure:**
- One sentence: observation + your experience
- Two sentences: agree/react, then add context from your practice
- Never: claim → elaborate → conclude (formal essay style)

**Comment content rule:** Comments must not endorse or softly question the target post. Endorsements ("great point", "data backs this up") give the author nothing to respond to. Soft questions directed at a brand account ("curious how X handles Y") are treated as noise and generate no reply. Every comment must either (a) name a tension or limitation the original post didn't raise, or (b) extend the argument with a specific NZ primary care example. If a genuine contribution is not possible for a given target, replace the target rather than posting an endorsement.

### Target selection priority

- **Tier 1 (preferred):** NZ-based individuals or organisations active in health, health tech, or primary care policy — these audiences have direct context for Ryo's NZ perspective.
- **Tier 2 (acceptable):** Global health tech accounts discussing problems where NZ has specific comparative evidence (small-country rollout, Medtech/PMS integration, PHO-scale deployment).
- **Deprioritise:** US-headquartered brand accounts (Abridge, Nuance, Epic), personal disclosure posts, job postings, and academic research with no NZ application pathway.
- If fewer than 4 Tier 1/2 targets are available from the personal feed, use hashtag fallback on #nzhealthtech, #nzprimarycare, or #generalpractice.

## Hashtag Strategy (2025 Algorithm)

**LinkedIn's algorithm changed in 2025:** Hashtags now matter less than keywords and engagement signals. Use 3-4 hashtags maximum per post. More than 5 gets penalised.

**Consistent set (use in every post):**
- #NewZealandGP
- #PrimaryHealthCare
- #MedtechNZ

**Add one context-specific tag per post:**
- #HealthTech (for infrastructure/technical posts)
- #DigitalHealth (for building-in-public posts)
- #GeneralPractice (for policy/workforce posts)
- #NZHealth (for broader health system posts)

**Don't:**
- Use more than 4 hashtags per post
- Use generic hashtags like #Innovation #AI #Healthcare
- Use hashtags to reach irrelevant audiences
- Create custom hashtags (waste of space)

**Why this matters:**
- Algorithm prioritises keywords in post text over hashtags
- Hashtags still signal topic relevance to algorithm
- Consistent hashtag use builds topic authority
- 3-4 tags is sweet spot (research shows 5+ reduces reach by 68%)

## Tagging Strategy (2025 Algorithm Rules)

**Critical: The algorithm now actively penalises irrelevant tagging as spam.**

**Safe tagging rules:**
- Tag 1-3 people or companies maximum per post
- Only tag when legitimately relevant (discussing their work, platform, policy)
- Never tag hoping to generate engagement
- Never mass-tag to expand reach

**Examples of legitimate tagging:**
- Tagging Medtech when analysing their API update
- Tagging RNZCGP when discussing their policy position
- Tagging a GP who gave you product feedback in a building-in-public post

**Examples of spam tagging:**
- Tagging 10 GPs hoping they'll engage
- Tagging practice managers you don't know
- Tagging companies unrelated to post content

**Research finding:** Tagging 5+ people or tagging irrelevant people triggers spam filters and kills post reach immediately.

**Never tag more than 1-3 people per comment.** Never comment generically.

## Algorithm Penalty Triggers (What Not to Do)

LinkedIn's 2025 algorithm actively penalises:

1. **Engagement baiting:** Asking "what do you think?" or similar questions to force comments
2. **Link stuffing:** Outbound links in post body (reduces initial reach by 30%)
3. **Excessive hashtags:** More than 5 hashtags per post
4. **Irrelevant tagging:** Tagging people without genuine reason
5. **Posting too frequently:** Less than 12 hours between posts
6. **Low-quality content:** Posts with grammar errors, spam-like formatting
7. **Overpromising:** Mentioning features that don't exist yet
8. **Engagement pods:** Coordinated likes/comments from same group (algorithm detects this)

**Don't:**
- Open posts with product name or update
- Ask open-ended questions about workflow problems (invites competitors)
- Put links in post body (first comment only)
- Post more than once per week until network is bigger
- Use more than 4 hashtags
- Tag people without genuine reason

**Penalties to avoid:**
- Posting more than once per week (splits engagement)
- Posting and disappearing (kills algorithmic momentum)
- Engagement pods (algorithm detects coordinated activity)
- Overpromising features that do not exist
- Using third-party scheduling tools that post via API rather than browser (penalised by LinkedIn's Nexus algorithm in 2025)
